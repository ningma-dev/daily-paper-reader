# DexSynRefine: Synthesizing and Refining Human-Object Interaction Motion for Physically Feasible Dexterous Robot Actions 

Hyesung Lee 1,2,∗, Hyunwoo Jung 3, Si-Hwan Heo 1, Sungwook Yang 11Korea Institute of Science and Technology, 2KAIST, 3Hanyang University 

https://dexsynrefine.github.io/ 

Abstract: Learning dexterous manipulation from human-object interaction (HOI) data is a scalable alternative to teleoperation, but HOI demonstrations are sparse and provide only kinematic motion that is not directly executable under embodi-ment mismatch and contact-rich dynamics. We present DexSynRefine , a frame-work with three coupled components: HOI-MMFP, a task- and object-initial-state-conditioned extension of motion manifold primitives that synthesizes coordinated hand-object trajectories from sparse HOI demonstrations; a task-space residual RL policy that physically grounds the synthesized reference while inheriting its kinematic structure; and a contact-and-dynamics adaptation module that enables sim-to-real transfer from proprioceptive history. Across five dexterous manipu-lation tasks spanning pick-and-place, tool use, and object reorientation, our task-space residual policy outperforms prior action-representation baselines in simu-lations and transfers to a real robot on all five tasks, improving over kinematic retargeting by 50 –70 percentage points. 

Keywords: Dexterous Manipulation, Motion Synthesizing, Learning from Hu-man Data, Reinforcement Learning

Recent advances in imitation learning [1–4] have significantly improved multi-fingered dex-terous manipulation, enabling increasingly dex-terous and contact-rich robot behaviors learned from large-scale teleoperation datasets [5–8]. However, collecting such datasets remains ex-pensive, labor-intensive, and difficult to scale, especially for dexterous tasks that require co-ordinated finger motion, accurate contact tim-ing, and stable object control. Because human hands naturally produce exactly these contact-rich behaviors during everyday object interac-tion, a growing line of work has turned to more accessible recordings of human-object interac-tion (HOI)—ranging from Internet videos to motion-capture sessions [9–12]—as a substi-tute source of supervision. Yet converting human HOI data into executable dexterous robot behavior remains fundamen-tally difficult. Human demonstrations primarily

provide geometric or kinematic motion, while successful manipulation depends on contact forces, friction, and other aspects of contact-rich dynamics that are not directly observed. In addition, hu-man and robot hands differ substantially in kinematic structure, actuation, and contact geometry, creating a large embodiment gap between observed human motion and feasible robot action. Recent work has mitigated this issue by using human or object motion as references for goal-conditioned reinforcement learning [13–18]. However, these methods still rely on suitable reference interactions for each task instance. Since HOI datasets are inherently sparse, such references may not exist when the task configuration or initial object state departs from the training distribution. The key chal-lenge is therefore not only to physically refine a given human prior, but also to construct task and state-generalizable interaction priors from sparse HOI data. We address this challenge with DexSynRefine , a framework that learns task and object initial state conditioned HOI motion priors from sparse demonstrations and grounds them into deployable dex-terous actions. We instantiate the prior as HOI-MMFP, an extension of Motion Manifold Flow Primitives (MMFP) [19, 20] that synthesizes interaction trajectories conditioned on task identity and initial object state. The synthesized trajectories are then refined through task-space residual re-inforcement learning to produce physically feasible behavior in simulation under embodiment mis-match and contact-rich dynamics. While the residual policy yields physically feasible behavior in simulation, real-robot deployment introduces an additional challenge: object properties and contact states observable in simulation are unavailable from onboard sensing. Prior adaptation methods such as RMA [21, 22] infer hidden dynamics from proprioceptive history, but dexterous execution also depends on accurately responding to rapid finger-object contact transitions. DexSynRefine therefore couples a contact estimator with a dynamics latent inferred from proprioceptive history, improving real-world robustness. Our contributions are threefold: (1) a task and state-conditioned generative prior for coupled hand-object interaction trajectories, (2) a residual RL formulation that physically grounds synthesized HOI motion under embodiment and contact constraints, supported by a systematic comparison of action representations identifying task-space residuals as the most effective formulation, and (3) a contact and dynamics aware adaptation mechanism for robust sim-to-real transfer of dexterous manipulation policies. 

2 Related Works 

Augmenting HOI Motion Prior with Goal Conditioned RL. A promising direction to bridge kinematic human motion and dynamic robot execution is to use HOI demonstrations as priors and learn an absolute or residual policy via reinforcement learning. Existing formulations vary in their action representation: Zhao et al. [15] learn a joint-space residual added to a retargeted primitive; Li et al. [14] pre-train an imitation policy and then fine-tune a residual policy for physical interaction; Chen et al. [13] adopt a hierarchical design with a high-level wrist-imitation policy and a low-level RL policy that mixes task-space wrist residuals with absolute finger commands; and Lum et al. [18] learn an absolute joint-action policy on top of a geometric fabric controller, using human hand kinematics only in the pre-manipulation phase. However, these methods rely on sparse HOI demonstrations, and which action representation is most effective for HOI-conditioned RL has not been systematically studied. They also require a matching reference at deployment, which breaks under out-of-distribution initial object poses. Concurrent HOI generative models provide motion priors but focus on unconditional or text-conditioned gener-ation, lacking the joint conditioning on task and initial object pose required for online deployment. We therefore propose Human-Object Interaction Motion Manifold Flow Primitives (HOI-MMFP), which jointly synthesizes coherent hand and object trajectories from initial conditions for dense RL guidance. Our systematic comparison further identifies task-space residual RL on both wrist and fingers as the most effective formulation for bridging the embodiment gap. 23 Method 

3.1 Task Setup 

We consider the problem of transferring sparse human-object interaction (HOI) demonstrations into executable robot behavior for dexterous manipulation. Each demonstration is represented as 

τ = {(wt, ht, ot)}Tt=1 , (1) where wt ∈ SE(3) and ot ∈ SE(3) denote the wrist pose and object pose in the world frame, respectively, and ht ∈ RK×3 denotes the K hand keypoints expressed in the wrist frame. Each trajectory is associated with a task condition c = ( u, o0), where u is a task embedding and o0 is the initial object pose. Given a sparse HOI demonstration set D = {(τ (i), c(i))}Ni=1 , our goal is to learn a dexterous robot policy that generalizes to unseen initial object poses. DexSynRefine addresses this problem in three stages: (1) object-centric augmentation of sparse HOI trajectories, (2) task- and state-conditioned HOI motion prior synthesis via HOI-MMFP, and (3) residual policy learning with deployment-oriented contact and dynamics adaptation. 

3.2 HOI Motion Collection and Object-Centric Augmentation 

We collect seven HOI demonstrations per task for five dexterous manipulation tasks ( Reorient Pringles Bottle, Pick Up and Hammer, Pick and Pour Watering Can, Pick and Place Book, Pick and Place Bowl ) spanning pick-and-place, tool use, and object reorientation, using a multi-camera setup with FoundationPose [23] and Manus Gloves, varying only the object’s initial yaw. To generalize from such sparse data, we apply object-centric augmentation: initial object poses are perturbed by planar SE(2) transformations, with the reaching phase interpolated and the ma-nipulation phase rigidly transformed to preserve hand-object structure. For symmetric objects, we additionally randomize rotations around the symmetry axis. This yields approximately 300 aug-mented trajectories per task, each resampled to a fixed horizon of T = 220 . Details are provided in Appendix A. 

3.3 HOI-MMFP: Task-Conditioned HOI Motion Prior Synthesis 

HOI-MMFP extends Motion Manifold Flow Primitives (MMFP) to human-object interaction by synthesizing trajectories conditioned on task identity and initial object state. The overall training pipeline of HOI-MMFP is illustrated in Figure 2(a). The training of HOI-MMFP proceeds in two decoupled stages: manifold learning and conditional latent flow matching. In the first stage, given an augmented trajectory τ aug , we learn a latent HOI motion manifold using a Transformer-based autoencoder, where the encoder maps τ aug to a latent code z∗ = E(τ aug ) and the decoder reconstructs it as ˜τ = D(z∗). To ensure a well-structured and temporally continuous latent space, the autoencoder is optimized with a combined loss: 

LAE = Lrecon + ηLnorm + δLsmooth , (2) where Lrecon = ∥τ aug − ˜τ ∥22 is the standard reconstruction error, and Lnorm = ∥z∗∥22 regularizes the magnitude of the latent codes. Furthermore, to enforce kinematic continuity across the manifold, we introduce a smoothness regularization Lsmooth . Specifically, we decode linearly interpolated latent codes τ mix = D(αz∗ + (1 − α)zj ), where zj is a randomly permuted latent code within the batch and α ∼ U (αmin , α max ). Lsmooth then minimizes the squared temporal differences of these interpolated trajectories, ensuring smooth transitions between states. To enable task-conditioned generation on the learned manifold, we train a conditional flow-matching model in latent space, using the task condition c = ( u, o0) defined in Sec 3.1, where u is extracted by a pretrained text encoder. 3.4 Task-Space Residual Action Learning for Physically Feasible Execution 

Although HOI-MMFP produces kinematically plausible interaction trajectories, the synthesized mo-tion is not directly executable on the robot due to embodiment mismatch and contact-rich physical constraints. We therefore learn a residual policy that refines the synthesized task-space references into physically feasible behavior, as illustrated in Figure 2(b). At the beginning of each episode, we randomize the initial object pose and query HOI-MMFP once to produce ˆτ ; subsequent control steps reuse this fixed reference.

4 Experiments and Results 

We design our experiments to answer four key questions, each corresponding to a core component of DexSynRefine: (Q1) Does HOI-MMFP synthesize human-object interaction (HOI) trajectories that are smoother, more spatially generalizable, and more effective as references for downstream RL compared to existing generative baselines? (Q2) Which action representation is most effective for grounding these HOI references into physically feasible robot behaviors? (Q3) Does jointly adapting object dynamics and contact states from proprioceptive history outperform single-modality adaptation? (Q4) Does the full DexSynRefine pipeline successfully transfer to a real robot system? To address these questions, we evaluate our approach across the five dexterous manipulation tasks. Unless otherwise specified, all simulation results report the mean and root mean square error eval-uated across 100 environments, 10 trials, and 10 random seeds. Performance metrics include the success rate (SR) at termination, as well as the object translation (OTE, in meters) and orientation (OOE, in degrees) errors averaged over the entire episode. 

4.1 Motion Synthesis Quality and Downstream RL Usability Baselines. We compare HOI-MMFP against a Task-Conditioned VAE (TC-VAE) [27], which gen-erates trajectories by combining a task-independent latent representation with explicit task condi-tioning, and a full-trajectory variant (DiT-Full) that applies our flow-matching backbone directly to the trajectory space, ablating the learned motion manifold. Full architectural and training details for all three priors are provided in Appendix B. We evaluate (i) spatial generalization under initial-pose perturbations, and (ii) motion smoothness and its utility for downstream RL refinement.

Results. The Full teacher achieves the highest success rate and lowest errors while both ablations nearly fail, showing that contact and dynamics signals are jointly indispensable. 5 Conclusion 

We presented DexSynRefine , a framework that converts sparse HOI demonstrations into deploy-able dexterous robot behavior through three coupled components: HOI-MMFP, a task- and state-conditioned generative manifold; task-space residual RL that physically grounds synthesized refer-ences under embodiment and contact constraints; and a contact-and-dynamics adaptation module enabling sim-to-real transfer from proprioception alone. Across five tasks spanning pick-and-place, tool use, and object reorientation, our pipeline outperforms prior action representations in simula-tion and transfers successfully to a real robot on all five tasks, improving over kinematic retargeting by 50 –70 percentage points. These results suggest that combining structured generative motion pri-ors with task-space residual refinement is a promising path toward learning dexterous manipulation from sparse human data. 

6 Limitations 

We identify three key limitations of DexSynRefine. (i) Per-task residual policies. Because each task involves distinct contact patterns and object dynamics, we currently train a separate residual policy for each task. Future work should aim to develop generalization capabilities that yield a single residual policy applicable to all tasks. (ii) Lossy contact reconstruction at deployment. 

Lacking tactile sensors, the student infers contact states (ˆbt, ˆft) from proprioception alone. This fails to capture rapid contact transitions, resulting in a 16 pp performance gap on the Hammer task (Sec 4.3). Equipping the hand with tactile fingertips would directly close this gap. (iii) Episode-start-only conditioning of the prior. HOI-MMFP synthesizes the trajectory ˆτ only once from the initial observation o0. Thus, if the object is displaced mid-execution—such as from external perturbations—the prior becomes stale and the system cannot reactively re-plan. Integrating HOI-MMFP with a Vision-Language Model (VLM) to perform periodic re-planning would allow the system to dynamically provide and update the target object trajectory. 8References

Object pose estimation. To obtain the object’s 6-DoF pose at every step we use FoundationPose, which requires an initial object mask. At each episode start we generate this mask by combining an open-vocabulary detection from Grounding DINO [31] with segmentation from SAM2 [32]; the resulting mask is supplied once to FoundationPose, which then tracks the object through the rest of the episode. 

Policy execution. In simulation the policy runs at the simulator step rate of 120 Hz, producing task-space targets that are then quintic-interpolated. On the real robot, policy queries are issued at 

20 Hz; their task-space targets are quintic-interpolated and streamed to libfranka at its 1 kHz real-time control loop. Because the generated reference trajectory does not always start at the robot’s current configuration, we move the robot to the initial pose of the generated trajectory before each episode and roll out the policy from there.

F Joint-Limit Compliance via Residual RL 

IK alone violates joint limits. We deliberately choose a Franka Panda base configuration that emulates the kinematic profile of a human arm so that wrist trajectories from the human-trained prior fall within the robot’s reachable workspace. Even with this alignment, tracking the wrist reference with damped least-squares (DLS) IK frequently drives the solved joint configuration into one or more joint limits before the wrist reaches its target, causing the planner to abort.
